# Literature Review Template

*A flexible fill-in template for course papers, thesis chapters, dissertations, and article introductions*

---

> **Note:** Use this template for a narrative or thematic literature review. Replace the bracketed prompts, delete sections you do not need, and adapt the headings to your discipline and citation style.

## Quick start

1. Fill in the focus-and-scope page before you draft.
2. Use the search log and source matrix while you read.
3. Turn the drafting section into themed subheadings rather than writing source by source.
4. Finish with the revision checklist before submission.

## Best fit

- Narrative / thematic reviews
- Thesis or dissertation literature chapters
- Research-paper introduction sections

> **Prompt:** If you are writing a systematic or scoping review, keep this structure but add protocol, eligibility, screening, and PRISMA reporting elements.

## 4. Drafting the review

Turn your strongest themes into section headings. A good review usually moves from context to comparison to evaluation to gap.

> **Paragraph pattern:** Make a claim about the literature -> compare two or more sources -> evaluate the quality, method, or context -> connect the paragraph to a larger trend, tension, or gap.

[Open with the topic and why it matters. Define the scope of the review, state the guiding question, and preview how the review is organized.]

## 5. Revision checklist

Use this before you submit. A literature review is strongest when the logic is visible on every page.

- [ ] Is my guiding question or problem clear in the introduction?
- [ ] Did I organize the body by themes, debates, methods, or trends rather than one source at a time?
- [ ] Does each major section synthesize multiple sources instead of only summarizing them?
- [ ] Did I evaluate the quality, scope, or limitations of the literature I cite?
- [ ] Did I include important disagreement, contradiction, or nuance?
- [ ] Are my scope and source-selection boundaries clear enough for a reader to follow?
- [ ] Do transitions explain why each section follows from the previous one?
- [ ] Does the conclusion state both what is known and what still needs research?
- [ ] Are in-text citations and the reference list fully consistent with the required style?
- [ ] Have I deleted all prompts, empty rows, and unused headings from this template?

## Optional add-ons for systematic or scoping reviews

Keep the main synthesis structure above, but also add the items below when your assignment expects formal evidence-synthesis reporting.

| Add | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Protocol / objective statement | Makes the review question, scope, and planned method explicit. |
| Eligibility criteria | Shows what was included and excluded. |
| Search details | Documents databases, dates, full strategies, and limits. |
| Screening / selection record | Shows how records moved from identification to inclusion. |
| PRISMA checklist / flow diagram | Provides a standard reporting structure for systematic and scoping reviews. |

> **Final cleanup:** Delete prompts, replace bracketed placeholders, remove blank rows, update headings, and make sure your references match the style required by your department or journal.
